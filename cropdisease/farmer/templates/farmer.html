Primary Crop Tomato
Pathogen Type Fungal

Late Blight

High Severity

Scientific name: Phytophthora infestans

Late blight is a devastating disease affecting tomatoes and potatoes worldwide. It spreads rapidly in cool, wet conditions and can destroy entire crops within days if left untreated.

Current Alert: High risk of late blight in your region due to recent rainfall and cool temperatures.
Common Symptoms
Dark, water-soaked leaf lesions White fungal growth under leaves Rapid defoliation Brown, firm fruit rot Distinct odor from infected plants
Favorable Conditions
Cool temperatures (10-24°C) High humidity (>90%) Frequent rainfall Poor air circulation
Recommended Treatments
Immediate Action (First 48 hours)
  • Remove and destroy all infected plants immediately
  • Apply copper-based fungicide (e.g., Bordeaux mixture) to all remaining plants
  • Treat surrounding plants preventatively even if not showing symptoms
Ongoing Treatment
  • Apply chlorothalonil or mancozeb every 7-10 days during wet periods
  • Alternate fungicides to prevent resistance
  • Use systemic fungicides like metalaxyl for severe infections
Organic Options
  • Baking soda spray (1 tbsp baking soda + 1 tsp oil + 1/2 tsp soap per gallon)
  • Neem oil applications every 5-7 days
  • Serenade Garden (Bacillus subtilis) foliar spray
Tip: Always apply treatments early in the morning or late in the evening to avoid leaf burn and maximize effectiveness.
Prevention Strategies
Cultural Practices
  • Plant resistant varieties when available (e.g., 'Mountain Magic', 'Defiant')
  • Ensure proper spacing (24-36 inches) for air circulation
  • Use drip irrigation instead of overhead watering
  • Rotate crops (3-4 year rotation away from tomatoes/potatoes)
  • Remove volunteer plants and nightshade weeds
Monitoring
  • Inspect plants daily during cool, wet weather
  • Use weather-based disease forecasting systems
  • Set up sentinel plants to detect early infections
Preventative Sprays
  • Begin preventative fungicides when weather favors disease
  • Apply protectant fungicides every 10-14 days in dry weather
  • Increase frequency to every 5-7 days during wet periods
  • Consider biofungicides like Bacillus subtilis for organic systems
Disease Lifecycle
Survival

The pathogen survives between crops in infected potato tubers, tomato debris, or volunteer plants.

Infection

Spores (sporangia) are produced on infected tissue and spread by wind, rain, or equipment. They germinate in free water on plant surfaces, penetrating directly or through stomata.

Development

Within 3-5 days under optimal conditions (18°C with 90% humidity), new lesions appear and produce more spores.

Global Distribution

Late blight occurs worldwide wherever tomatoes and potatoes are grown. It is particularly severe in:

  • Cool, humid regions of Europe and North America
  • Highland tropical areas
  • Coastal regions with frequent fog
New aggressive strains have emerged in recent years, showing resistance to some fungicides.
